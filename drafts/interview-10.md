## Major version transitions, long-lived branches, and production-equivalent-only environments

These notes come out of a real case: a Python library at a mocap studio, powering a bunch of desktop applications. Originally the library talked directly to the database and also carried a lot of business logic — part ORM, part application services. The current push is to decouple it from direct DB access: a web API has been stood up that owns the DB, and the library is supposed to go through an auto-generated OpenAPI client instead. They're on v2, the plan is a breaking v3 where DB access is gone. Real users, real teams, strict 4–6 week release cadence, a 2–3 day stage testing window before each release, no staging environment in the traditional sense (stage = the actual mocap stage, i.e. production with actors standing around). Slow-moving team, no AI allowed, just starting to adopt tooling like ruff. The lead's plan was to park all v3 work on a long-lived v3 branch and eventually merge it back. I pushed back on that, and the conversation surfaced a bunch of things the method currently doesn't address.

### What the method is missing

The biggest meta-point first. The whole method is tuned for a forgiving feedback loop — solo dev, small team, CI that fails before lunch, rollback is cheap. The rhythm of "small steps, merge often, squash merge, delete the branch" assumes that the cost of a bad change is measured in minutes to hours, not in "a room full of mocap actors standing around waiting for the pipeline to work." When the feedback loop gets expensive, slow, or risky, the shape of the guidance changes, and the method currently has very little to say about it. This case surfaced at least five distinct gaps, and they're all related enough that I think they belong in one section, but distinct enough to need individual names.

### Major version transitions deserve their own playbook

Right now the method treats versioning as a release-notes problem — it's mentioned in RELEASES.md and nowhere else. But a major version *transition* — the months-long period where you're preparing to break compatibility — is a workflow problem, not a release problem, and the method is silent on it. The principle I landed on in the conversation is that a major version bump should be the smallest, most boring commit in the release. If v3.0 is a scary release, you did the work wrong. The scary work should have happened incrementally in v2.x behind deprecation warnings and feature flags. The major version is the moment you *stop supporting the old thing*, not the moment you *build the new thing*. This reframes what a major version is *for* — it's the endpoint of a trail of breadcrumbs you've been leaving in the minor releases, not a container for risky work.

That principle has concrete implications. Deferred breaking changes should land on main during the v2.x cycle with `DeprecationWarning`s (or the language equivalent), each on their own release. Users get runway. Each breaking change gets validated in isolation. By the time v3 cuts, nothing in it is new — it's mostly just the release where deprecated things get deleted. I think this wants a short section in RELEASES.md or its own file under change-management. It pairs naturally with the next point.

### Long-lived branches have a legitimate exception but it's narrower than people assume

The method already gestures at this with "avoid long-running release branches unless you truly need parallel supported versions," but it doesn't say *when* that's actually true, and the lead's instinct to reach for a long-lived branch for a major version transition is a really common misreading. The refined guidance is: long-lived branches are justified when you need to *maintain* two versions in parallel — bugfixes to v2 while v3 is already live in the field. They are *not* justified for *building* the next version. Building the next version should happen on main, incrementally, behind gates.

The distinction is maintenance vs. construction. Parallel maintenance is a real use case and sometimes unavoidable. Parallel construction is drift waiting to happen — the longer the branch lives, the more main diverges, and the cost of reconciliation shows up at exactly the moment you can least afford surprises. The method should name this distinction explicitly because "long-lived v3 branch" is such a common and seductive wrong answer for anyone staring down a breaking change.

### Production-equivalent-only environments

This was the biggest surprise for me in the conversation. The method quietly assumes there's a staging step — somewhere safe to validate before real users see a change. Plenty of real systems don't have that. Mocap studios. Embedded devices shipped to the field. Industrial control systems. Research instruments. Game builds shipped to players. Scientific data pipelines. When stage *is* production, the guidance shifts in a way the method currently doesn't capture.

The short version: when there is no environment cheaper than production, the blast radius per change becomes the primary risk control. Flag-gated incremental rollout stops being a nice-to-have and becomes the default. A single cutover is almost never acceptable, because it concentrates all first-exposure risk into one moment. This reframes feature flags from "a thing teams sometimes use" to "the mechanism by which you earn the right to ship at all." The method currently treats flags as an implementation detail; for this class of environment, they're a structural requirement.

I think this is worth naming as its own category because the shape of the guidance is qualitatively different. You can't just tell someone in that situation to "merge often and iterate" — the iterate step isn't free, it costs them a shoot day. Everything has to be gated, everything has to be reversible, and the gates have to be at a granularity that matches the risk surface.

### Bounded validation windows

The client's 2–3 day stage testing window before each release is interesting because it's neither a persistent staging environment nor a naive "ship it and hope" model. It's a scheduled, time-boxed validation surface. A lot of slower-moving teams have something like this — pre-release soak periods, release candidate weeks, hardware bring-up windows, QA passes. The method doesn't have a vocabulary for this at all right now.

The guidance I want to capture: when validation is a scheduled activity rather than a continuous environment, flag lifecycles should map onto the cadence. Code can land in an "available but off" state during one window, get exercised during testing, and graduate to "default on" only after sustained confidence. The release cadence becomes the clock for rollout, not an obstacle to it. This was actually the insight that unlocked the whole reframe for the client — their release rhythm stopped being a constraint and started being the mechanism. Instead of fighting the cadence, we used it.

This feels like a small addition but I think it's a meaningful one. It gives slower teams a way to see their process as a resource to plan around rather than a limitation to route around. And it names something that a lot of teams already half-do without realizing they can lean on it structurally.

### Granularity of gates is a design axis

This one I've never seen written down anywhere and I think it's genuinely useful. The config discussion in this case surfaced something subtle: the *shape* of your feature gates matters a lot, and it's a design decision that should be made deliberately. An all-or-nothing flag and a per-resource flag look similar on paper but enable completely different rollout strategies. The client's current library had a global "DB or API" switch — a single boolean — and that global flag was structurally incompatible with the incremental migration I was proposing. The fix wasn't complicated (one new env var, a small helper module) but it was a prerequisite, and without naming it the whole reframe would have fallen apart.

The principle: the granularity of your feature gates determines the granularity of your rollouts. An all-or-nothing gate forces all-or-nothing decisions. When you need incremental rollout, the gate mechanism must be at least as fine-grained as the units you want to roll out independently. Design gates at the granularity of the *decisions you expect to make*, not the granularity of the *code they protect*.

This is a principle-level thing and I think it probably belongs in construction or workflow as general guidance, not buried in the major-version-transitions section. It applies way beyond this case — any time you're thinking about feature flags or config switches, the granularity choice is making rollout decisions for you whether you realize it or not.

### Working within constraints you didn't choose

This one is bigger than the others and might deserve its own separate strand of work. The method's current voice is optimized for someone making greenfield decisions or leading a small team that defers to their judgment. A lot of real work doesn't look like that. A lot of real work looks like: I'm one voice among several, my lead has already made some decisions I disagree with, the codebase has history I can't rewrite, ports-and-adapters is off the table because the architectural decision was made years ago, and I still need to do good work within that.

In this specific case I had to be very careful not to relitigate battles I'd already lost. The current library design uses ugly in-method if/else branches instead of proper ports and adapters, which I hated when it was introduced and still hate. But bundling "let's refactor to ports and adapters" into the v3 migration proposal would have poisoned the whole message — the lead would have heard "Gerardo wants to rewrite everything" and rejected the whole package. I had to explicitly concede the aesthetic battle to win the structural one, and the message I drafted deliberately didn't mention ports and adapters or call the if/else ugly. That's a different kind of judgment than the method currently trains for.

There's a related point about pitching. The reframe only worked because I could frame it as "same destination, safer path" rather than "your plan is wrong." I explicitly steelmanned the lead's concerns before answering them, gave a graceful off-ramp at the end ("if after thinking about it you still prefer the branch I'll execute that plan"), and positioned the config granularity change as a small prerequisite rather than a separate debate. None of that is in the method right now. The brownfield audit work is pointing in this direction but this is a different angle — brownfield audit is about technical constraints the codebase forces on you, this is about *political* constraints, what you can convince your lead of. The tactics are different. Worth distinguishing.

I don't know if this is a separate module or a cross-cutting concern that shows up in several places. My instinct is that it's cross-cutting — every module probably has something to say about "here's what this looks like when you have full control, and here's what it looks like when you don't." But that's a bigger rewrite than I want to commit to right now. For now I just want it on the record that the method has a blind spot here.

### What I'd actually add

If I were sitting down to write this up as a proper module, I'd bundle the first four points — major version transitions, the long-lived branch exception, production-equivalent-only environments, and bounded validation windows — into one short module or section. They're interrelated and they always show up together in real transitions. Something like "Breaking Changes at Scale" or "Major Version Transitions." The granularity-of-gates point is general enough that it probably belongs earlier, maybe in construction or workflow, as a standalone principle about feature gates.

The "working within constraints you didn't choose" theme needs its own thinking and I don't want to force it into this draft. It's adjacent to brownfield and adjacent to adoption but it's neither one. Parking that for later.

### Why this matters

The reason I want all of this captured is that the method right now is good at telling a solo dev or small team how to build software well in a forgiving environment. It has much less to say to someone working inside a larger, slower, higher-stakes system where decisions from years ago are still binding and the cost of being wrong is someone else's shoot day. That's a lot of real-world engineering. Not all of it — the method is still right about the things it addresses — but a meaningful chunk of it. Every time I hit one of these cases in consulting work, it reveals another gap, and the gaps are starting to cluster around a recognizable theme: the method assumes the feedback loop is cheap, and when it isn't, the shape of the right answer changes.

This interview is a data point in that direction. The brownfield audit work was another. Mixed-skill teams was another. I think there's a whole companion strand of the method waiting to be written that addresses the slower, messier, more constrained end of the spectrum — not as an afterthought or an exception but as a first-class mode of operation. Whether that's a new module, a cross-cutting section, or a different book entirely, I don't know yet. But the pattern is real and it's showing up consistently.
